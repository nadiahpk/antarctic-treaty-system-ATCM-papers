I.    BACKGROUND

As a complement to Item 9b "Inspections under the Antarctic Treaty. Inspection
Checklist", submitted by the United Kingdom at the XIXth Antarctic Treaty Consultative
Meeting, Chile wishes to take up that initiative and describe its experience with inspections of
ships setting out to the Antarctic, under the rules of international conventions and effective
domestic maritime regulations.

II.    REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

A.    International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, 1973,
      amended by the 1978 Protocol (MARPOL 73/78)

B.    International Convention for the Safety of Human Life at Sea. (SOLAS 74)

C.    Chilean Navigation Law, Regulations for the control of water pollution

III.    IMPLEMENTATION

A.    MARPOL INTERNATIONAL CONVENTION, 73/78

      1.    Scope

            1.1    Applicable to ships that have the right to fly the flag of one of the
                   Convention's parties.

            1.2    Applicable to ships that do not have the right to fly the flag of one
                   of the parties, but which operate under the authority of a State
                   party to the Convention.

            1.3    Not applicable to warships or to ancillary naval units; neither is
                   it applicable to ships which, even though the property of a State
                   or at its service, are rendering temporary government services of
                   a non commercial nature. Notwithstanding the above, each Party
                   must ensure that the appropriate measures are taken so that ships
                   that are owned by, or at the service of the State, act in accordance
                   with the Convention's purposes and goals.

      2.    Applicable regulations, marpol convention, 73/78

            2.1    Annex I - Rules to prevent pollution produced by oil

            2.2    Annex V - Rules to prevent pollution produced by waste
2

3.    Inspection of vessels

3.1   All vessels must carry a certificate of compliance with each rule,
      which will have to be shown to an inspector of the Chilean
      General Directorate of the Maritime Territory and Merchant
      Marine when at offshore ports and terminals. If there are any
      questions regarding the status of a vessel or its equipment, the
      vessel will be unable to set sail until the threat to the marine
      environment has been resolved. This decision shall be made
      known to the vessel's State of registration, through its consular
      representative and the International Maritime Organization's
      Secretariat.

3.2   Chile also carries out inspections of vessels from States not parties
      to the MARPOL 73/78 Convention or to the Antarctic Treaty, so
      as to ensure non preferential treatment of these vessels.

B.    INTERNATIONAL CONVENTION FOR THE SAFETY OF HUMAN LIFE
      AT SEA, SOLAS 74

1.    Scope

1.1   Applicable to all vessels on international trips, except:
      
      •    Warships
      •    Cargo ships under 500 DWT
      •    Pleasure yachts not used for commercial purposes
      •    Fishing vessels

2.    Applicable regulations

2.1   Chapter III   Rescue Equipment
2.2   Chapter IV    Radiotelephony
2.3   Chapter V    Navigation Safety
2.4   Chapter VII   Transportation of Hazardous Materials

3.    Inspection of vessels

      Administrative Inspection of the following certificates by an Inspector of
      the Chilean General Directorate of the Maritime Territory and Merchant
      Marine:

3.1   Safety certificate for passenger ships
3.2   Construction safety certificate for cargo ships
3

3.3 Equipment safety certificate for cargo ships
3.4 Radiotelegraphy safety certificate for cargo ships
3.5 Radiotelephony safety certificate for cargo ships

If there are any questions regarding the status of a vessel or its equipment, the vessel will be unable to set sail until it can do so without endangering passengers or crew. This decision shall be made known to the vessel's State of registration, through its consular representative and the International Maritime Organization's Secretariat.

C.  CHILEAN NAVIGATION LAW, REGULATIONS FOR THE CONTROL 
OF WATER POLLUTION

1.  Scope

    1.1 Applicable to all vessels, regardless of their size and purpose, including fishing and sports vessels, except for warships, and vessels at the service of a State.

2.  Applicable titles

    TITLE I:   General Provisions
    TITLE II:  On Ships
    TITLE VI:  Penalties and Fines

3.  Inspection of vessels

By an Inspector of the Chilean General Directorate of the Maritime Territory and Merchant Marine, who will make an administrative check of the certificates required by the regulations, and question the vessel's Captain on his knowledge of the Antarctic Treaty in regards to ecosystem conservation, in particular on the pollution produced by oil and waste. Were the inspection to detect deficiencies in the status of the vessel or its equipment, it will be unable to set sail until these have been resolved.

Introduction

The following pages of this section show a comprehensive list of items of machinery space operations which are, when appropriate, to be recorded in the Oil Record Book in accordance with regulation 20 of Annex 1 of the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, 1973, as modified by the Protocol of 1978 relating thereto (MARPOL 73/78). •	When making entries in the Oil Record Book, the date, operational code and item number shall be inserted in the appropriate columns and the required particulars shall be recorded chronologically in the blank spaces.

Each completed operation shall be signed for and dated by the officer or officers in charge. Each completed page shall be signed by the master of the ship.

The Oil Record Book contains many references to oil quantity. The limited accuracy of tank measurement devices, temperature variations and clingage will affect the accuracy of these readings. The entries in the Oil Record Book should be considered accordingly.
