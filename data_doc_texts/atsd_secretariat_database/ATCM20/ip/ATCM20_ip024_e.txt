MARITIME SAFETY IN THE ANTARCTIC

I.    Introduction

During its forty nine years of active presence in the Antarctic, and through the Chilean 
General Directorate of the Maritime Territory and Merchant Marine Services, the Chilean Navy 
has contributed to Maritime Safety by: protecting routes, through maritime signals; cartography; 
sending urgent messages to navigators; meteorological information; certification of the 
professional qualification of captains and crews; certification of safety and pollution control 
equipment on board ships; training of, and services by, Antarctic pilots; maritime 
communications; inspection and safety certification of ships; control of maritime traffic, and 
search and rescue activities, for safeguarding of lives, damage prevention of ships and their 
cargo, and protection of the Antarctic ecosystem.

The Chilean General Directorate of the Maritime Territory and Merchant Marine, in order 
to provide effective services, is divided into 16 Maritime Administrations organized along its 
coasts. One of them is the Antarctic Maritime Administration, located at "Capitán Arturo Prat" 
Naval Base, on Greenwich Island, which oversees the operations of the port authorities of Fildes 
Bay, Sovereignty Bay, Covadonga Anchorage and Paradise Bay.

These harbour authorities are usually activated during the time of the year with the 
greatest maritime traffic, i.e., between October 15 and March 15. They act as coordinators of 
search and rescue operations, oil spills control and containment. They also rescue ships involved 
in accidents, while continuously monitoring maritime traffic to render assistance when necessary, 
whether with its own resources or with the civilian resources closest to the area of the accident.

During the 1994/1995 summer season, 48 ships of all types and flags were monitored; 
during the 1995/1996 summer season, 53 ships were monitored.

II.   Chile's contribution to maritime safety in the following areas:

A.    Crews

1.    Maritime Training Center

The mission of the Maritime Training Center (CIMAR) is to improve the 
professional qualifications of the Merchant Marine, fishing, tourism and recreation 
personnel, in accordance with the requirements stipulated by domestic regulations and by 
international conventions signed by Chile at the International Maritime Organization.

CIMAR is a professional training center recognized by the International Maritime 
University of Malmo, Sweden.

2.    The "Piloto Pardo" Antarctic Navigation School

Aware of the difficulties involved in navigating the Antarctic, the Chilean Navy 
operates the "Piloto Luis Pardo Villalón" Antarctic Navigation School, established in
2

September 1991. The School offers a course on Navigation in Antarctic Waters, taken 
by Chilean captains navigating in the high-seas, in order to qualify them to take charge 
of ships operating in that area.

During the School's five years of existence, 36 professionals have been qualified; 
they have piloted four ships flying other flags at the request of their respective shipowners 
and under the orders of the Chilean Maritime Authority.

3.    Maritime Search and Rescue Services

From the time of the historic rescue of Sir Ernest Shackleton's expedition from 
Elephant Island, on August 30, 1916, carried out by the Coast Guard cutter "YELCHO", 
under the command of its pilot Luis Pardo Villalón, and up to the present, the Chilean 
Maritime Search and Rescue Service has been activated on several occasions to render 
assistance to ships that have suffered accidents, both at the Drake Passage and near the 
Antarctic Peninsula; it has been able to rescue their crews, ships and cargo, thus 
preventing damages to the Antarctic environment.

One of the functions of the Search and Rescue Operations Center, with 
headquarters at Punta Arenas, is to coordinate with its respective subcenters (Port 
Williams-Cape Horn, Fildes Bay and Prat Base) all operations aimed at prompt arrival 
to the accident site.

These activities have been made possible through the operations of the Naval 
Antarctic Patrol (PAN). PAN has two ships capable of remaining for long periods of time 
in the Antarctic; they carry out search and rescue activities. In addition, they are 
equipped to carry out search and rescue operations; control marine pollution caused by 
oil spills; provide logistic support to other ships, and act as a safety escort for sport 
navigation activities. Both ships, one of them south of Parallel 57°S and the other in the 
Cape Horn area, are in permanent state of readiness.

B.     Routes

1.    Maritime Signalling Services

In this area of maritime safety, a signalling network has been set up which 
contributes effectively to the safety of Antarctic navigation; it includes 20 light beacons, 
80 "blind" beacons and three racons, which are described in the attached annexes.

2.    Hydrographic and Oceanographic Services

          Cartography

The publication of Chilean Antarctic charts began with the first Antarctic 
expedition in 1946. Through its hydrographic activities, it has been able to develop an 
Antarctic File which assists users in the area of navigational safety.
3

Under its agreement with the International Hydrographic Organization, regarding 
Antarctic hydrographic cooperation, Chile is bringing up to date its nautical charts; it is 
responsible for preparing 14 charts based on new cartography guidelines.

The attached annexes show Chile’s current Antarctic Nautical Charts, as well as 
those for which it will be responsible between 1996 and the year 2000.

Oceanography

Regarding maritime safety in the Antarctic Peninsula, we wish to emphasize that 
Chile has complemented its new hydrographic and cartography work with the 
oceanographic monitoring and research activities carried out since 1959.

In 1991 Chile began participating in the World Ocean Circulation Experiment 
Program (WOCE), installing a tide station at the Diego Ramírez islands and carrying out 
WOCE cruises at the Drake Passage, so as to determine “geostrophic flows” in order to 
study climate changes.

Another permanent tide station was installed at the Arturo Prat Naval Base, aimed 
at assisting in the operations of the Tidal Wave Warning System and the Mean Sea-Level 
Program.

3.    Maritime Communications Services

The Antarctic Peninsula and its navigation route connecting it to the American continent 
is covered by a network of fixed stations operated by Communications Area Centers located in 
the Maritime Administrations of Punta Arenas, Port Williams (Cape Horn) and the Antarctic 
(Prat Base). Urgent news are sent from these stations to navigators. They are open 24 hours a 
day to answer SOS calls; they provide these public services to ships navigating in the area.

4.    Meteorological Services

The Meteorology Post in Punta Arenas, assisted by the fixed and mobile stations located 
in Cape Horn and Drake Passage, respectively, as well as in the Antarctic Peninsula, provide 
local meteorological forecasts to navigators sailing in the Antarctic Peninsula, including the 
following reports:

•    Area Analysis of the Antarctic region.

•    Weekly dissemination of the glaciological conditions of bays, inlets and straits.

•    Meteorological forecasts for Antarctic Area IX.

•    Meteorological information based on navigators’ specific needs

5.    Antarctic contingency plan applicable to oil spills

As a result of recent ship accidents in the Antarctic, as well as the continuous increase 
in maritime traffic produced by numerous scientific and tourist expeditions which reflect 
mankind’s increasing interest in the Antarctic, the countries operating in that area have become 
extremely interested in protecting its ecosystem.

Accordingly, Chile’s Navy has been organized and trained to carry out oil spill 
emergency operations throughout its territory. These operations fall under the authority of the 
General Directorate of Maritime Territory and Merchant Marine; the Directorate is also the 
national coordinating center for pollution control (with headquarters in Valparaíso). For 
implementation purposes, there is a regional pollution control center in the Antarctic area, in 
Punta Arenas, in charge of the following fixed and mobile sub-centers and their equipment:

Fixed Sub-centers

•    Port Authority of Punta Arenas
•    Port Authority of Port Williams
•    Port Authority of Fildes Bay
•    Port Authority of Prat Base

Mobile Sub-centers

•    Naval Antarctic Patrol: two high-seas tugboats
•    “Almirante Viel” icebreaker
Equipment

·   Containment barriers
·   Mechanical oil recovery equipment
·   Sorbent material
·   Manoeuvring equipment, and fixtures for cleanup of coasts and polluted flora and 
    fauna

C.  Transportation

1.  Ship Inspection Services

The mission of Ship Inspection Services is to check the operational status of cargo 
and/or passenger ships, naval, fishing and sports vessels' gear, through inspection and 
certification of hulls and safety equipment in general. For this purpose, in each of the 
Chilean ports a corps of specialized inspectors monitors decks, telecommunications, and 
naval machinery and construction; they carry out their inspections in accordance with 
domestic maritime standards and/or international conventions.
