





Data Collection and Reporting on Yachting Activity in Antarctica





Data Collection and Reporting on Yachting Activity in Antarctica
Working Paper submitted by the United States and the United Kingdom
1. Introduction
The Committee for Environmental Protection (CEP) XII endorsed a comprehensive study to assess the status, development and environmental impact of tourism and non-governmental activities in the Antarctic.  This study commenced in May 2008 and is anticipated to conclude before the Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting (ATCM) XXXIV.  A detailed progress report on this project is provided in a separate Working Paper (Environmental Aspects and Impacts of Tourism and Non-governmental Activities in Antarctica: Project Report), submitted to CEP XIII by New Zealand.  
As part of this study, attempts were made to compile records on yachting activity in the Antarctic.  Considerable difficulties were encountered with regard to data compilation and validation as existing records are largely incomplete and inconsistent.  While IAATO collates detailed data on their yacht members, it appears that IAATO yachts comprise only a small component of the Antarctic yachting community. In order to offer a more complete picture of yachting activities in Antarctica and to be able to assess more adequately potential environmental risks, the Treaty Parties are asked to contribute further information on yachting that would add to the data provided in this Working Paper.
2. Data compiled on yachting in the Antarctic
The most complete records on yacht tourism exist for Port Lockroy, where the United Kingdom (UK) has been administering an extensive visitor monitoring scheme (for all vessels and visitors) over the last 14 seasons.  Nonetheless, any conclusion on yachting activities in the Antarctic will be severely constrained by the limited data available.  Although it is assumed that the majority of yachts visiting the Antarctica Peninsula pass through Port Lockroy, additional information is necessary before an assessment of the potential environmental risks associated with Antarctic yachting yields substantive results. 
Within the boundaries of the information currently available, the Port Lockroy data can be used to give a rough indication of the extent of yacht tourism in Antarctica. In addition to the Port Lockroy data, the CEP tourism study has compiled a very sparsely populated list of yachts used by private expeditions or, to a lesser extent, commercial tourism operators in Antarctica (see Appendix A).  This list needs to be updated and completed before further assessments of yachting in Antarctica can take place.  
3. Recommendations
The Treaty Parties are asked to contribute information that will add to the data presented in Appendix A or to yachting activities in Antarctica in general.  In addition to the headings currently listed in Appendix A, it would be important for the records to include the following data about the yachts:
    • Construction material (e.g., metal, wood, fibreglass),
    • Whether operated as a charter or private expedition,
    • If a charter, who was the operator,
    • Indication if IAATO member or not, and
    • The seasons during which the yacht operated in Antarctica.
Contributions that address this matter are warmly welcomed. Dr. Neil Gilbert (New Zealand) has agreed to be the Point of Contact to receive information at n.gilbert@antarcticanz.govt.nz
