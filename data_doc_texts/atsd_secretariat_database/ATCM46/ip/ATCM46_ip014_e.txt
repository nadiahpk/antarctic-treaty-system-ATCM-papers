Consistency in animal spatial tracking for monitoring Antarctic top
predators
Consistency in seabirds spatial tracking for monitoring Antarctic top
predators
An Information Paper submitted by Portugal, Canada, France,
Japan, Korea (ROK), New Zealand, the United Kingdom and the
United States
Summary
Tracking information can help Parties to monitor Antarctic animal
populations to support policies on conservation. Animal tracking
provides valuable data on the distribution of animals but can be
costly and logistically demanding to Antarctic national
programmes. Scientific evidence using tracking data from seabird
species (including Antarctic species), collected between 2002-2020,
showed that collecting tracking data from one year can be sufficient
to understand their distribution across years. Such scientific
information can be relevant to aid monitoring programs involving
tracking of Antarctic species, specifically allowing the identification
of important sites for seabirds on foraging/feeding areas, threats at
sea and be a step towards an effective network of marine protected
areas.
Introduction
The Committee for Environmental Protection (CEP) called for
research and monitoring to improve the basis for decision-making,
and prioritizing the work of the Subsidiary Group on Climate
Change Response (SGCCR) and, in particular, continued progress on
the Climate Change Response Works Programme (CCRWP)
(Resolution 4 (2015) (ATCM XLII Final Report, agenda item 7,
Paragraph 48). Top predators, such as seabirds (e.g., penguins and
albatrosses), have been used to monitor Southern Ocean ecosystems
(Agnew 1997, Watters et al. 2020), with tracking data proving
valuable for the identification and protection of areas of ecological
significance (Hindell et al. 2020, Ropert-Coudert et al. 2020).
Tracking has provided unique insights on the distribution of
animals at sea. However, animal tracking can be costly and
logistically demanding, particularly in the Antarctic. Therefore, it is
important to understand how many years of data is necessary to
capture changes in distribution in response to varying
environmental conditions. Such work has been relevant to bodies,
such as the Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research (SCAR), the
Agreement for the Conservation of Albatrosses and Petrels (ACAP)
and the Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Living
Resources (CCAMLR), as stated in the CEP Five-year Work Plan
(ATCM XLVI Final Report).
In this paper, based on research by scientists from Portugal,
Australia, Canada, France, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand, the
United Kingdom and the United States on tracking of seabird
species worldwide, including from the Antarctic region (i.e.,
wandering albatross (Diomedea exulans), black-browed albatross
(Thalassarche melanophris), grey-headed albatross (Thalassarche
chrysostoma), chinstrap penguin (Pygoscelis antarctica)) was used
to assess the importance of annual variability in spatial distribution,
during their chick-rearing period, relevant for area-based
conservation (Beal et al. 2023).
Annual spatial consistency in seabirds
GPS tracking data from 23 seabird species around the world,
collected between 2002-2020 during chick-rearing period (when
seabirds are most spatially constrained), from multiple years
show that there is a high degree of spatial overlap among
distributions from different years in most species (Beal et al. 2023).
It was estimated that data from a single year would, on average,
miss only 5% of the full distribution (i.e., from data collected in 3
years, Figure 1)(Beal et al. 2023). For the Antarctic studied species,
there is some variation: chinstrap penguins (1-2% difference
between years) and wandering albatrosses (1-3%) are quite
consistent, while the grey-headed albatrosses (5-13%) are more
variable (Beal et al. 2023). The number of tracked animals (to
provide a representative estimate of the population distribution) is
the most important consideration, rather than tracking animals in
multiple years (Beal et al. 2023).
Figure 1. Comparison of the degree to which tracking samples from
differing numbers of years capture full multi-year population
distributions for XX seabird species. For each species, a certain
number of foraging trips, indicated by the number above the x-axis,
were re-sampled 100 times. bold text at the top of each panel
indicates where at least one test comparison was significant (1–2 y
or 1–3 y), with the value indicating the mean effect size between
comparisons. Panels correspond to the coverage for 95% (upper)
and 50% (lower) utilization distributions (Beal et al. 2023).
These results provide scientific evidence that the distribution of
Antarctic seabirds is similar between years and can help Parties to
monitor Antarctic seabird populations (including through tracking
methods) and their interactions with prey species to support
policies on marine area-based conservation. Such work can allow
the identification of important sites for seabirds on foraging/feeding
areas, threats at sea and be a step towards an effective network of
marine protected areas.
