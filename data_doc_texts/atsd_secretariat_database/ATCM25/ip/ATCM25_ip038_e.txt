Antarctic Protected Area System: Proposed Antarctic Specially Managed Area in 
the McMurdo Dry Valleys, Ross Sea Region

Information Paper Submitted to the Committee for Environmental Protection
submitted jointly by the United States and New Zealand
Antarctic Protected Areas System

Proposed Antarctic Specially Managed Area in the McMurdo Dry Valleys,
Southern Victoria Land

Information Paper Submitted Jointly by the United States and New Zealand

Background

The McMurdo Dry Valleys are located in Southern Victoria Land along the western coast
of McMurdo Sound, southern Ross Sea, at approximately 77°S, 162°E. The McMurdo
Dry Valleys are the largest ice-free region in Antarctica, with approximately thirty
percent of the ground surface largely free of snow and ice. The region encompasses the
southernmost cold desert ecosystem and its climate is cold, windy, and extremely arid.

The unique features of the McMurdo Dry Valleys, including its perennially frozen lakes,
watersheds, geological features, and sediments are of great scientific importance. The
McMurdo Dry Valleys are further regarded for their wilderness, aesthetic, and
educational values.

These features and values have meant that the McMurdo Dry Valleys are subject to
increasing visitation. Each year scientists, other national program personnel, and tour
groups visit the region. Increasing human activity and potentially conflicting interests
have made it is necessary to more effectively manage and coordinate activities within the
Area. As the main operators of national programs in the region, the United States and
New Zealand consider that Annex V of the Protocol and the design of the Antarctic
Specially Managed Area system provide an important mechanism for management of
activities in the McMurdo Dry Valleys region.

Development for the Draft Management Plan

The United States and New Zealand are jointly developing a draft management plan for
aproposed Antarctic Specially Managed Area (ASMA) for the McMurdo Dry Valleys.
Following two workshops in 1995 and 1998 on human impacts in the valleys, a joint
US/NZ workshop was held in August 2001 to progress development of the draft
management plan. The workshop included managers for both national programmes and
scientists active in the valleys. Much of the material from the earlier workshops was
drawn on in development of a first rough draft of the management plan and the associated
code of conduct. Since that time, a further draft been developed and circulated for review
by all those active within the Area as well as other interested groups.

The Area as it is currently drafted extends from the Convoy Range in the north to the
Alph River catchment in the south. The eastern boundary is the coast and the Area
includes most ice-free areas to the edge of the Polar Plateau in the west (see attached
map).
Objectives

The overall aim of the ASMA is to manage and coordinated human activities in the Area such that the values of the Area can be sustained in the long term. The specific draft objectives of management in the Area are to:

- Facilitate scientific research while maintaining stewardship of the environment;
- Assist with planning and coordination of all activities in the McMurdo Dry Valleys;
- Manage conflicts between different values and activities, including conflicts between different areas of scientific research, tour operations and scientific research, and education and scientific research.
- Ensure the long-term protection of ecosystem integrity and special features;
- Conserve the scientific, wilderness, ecological, aesthetic, and educational values of the Area;
- Minimize cumulative environmental impacts of human activities;
- Minimize the possibility of introduction of alien plants, animals and microbes to the Area;
- Promote the use of transportation modes having the least environmental impacts.
- Minimize the use of fossil fuels for the conduct of activities in the Area;
- Minimize the footprint of all facilities established in the Area, including the proliferation of field camps.

Management Approach

The management plan incorporates a zoning concept to manage activities and features in the landscape in a practical manner. Within the proposed Area, there are three types of zones: Facilities Zones, a Tourism Zone, and Special Features. This zoning concept manages multiple uses of, and activities in, the Area while providing protection for those features for which the Area is valued. Facilities Zones restrict areas of primary human activity, the Tourism Zone aims to further define and manage the area where tourism activities have traditionally occurred, and Special Features are established to provide special protection for particular environmental features of exceptional value. Specific guidelines for conduct of activities are associated with each zone.

The plan also includes two sets of guidelines: the Environmental Code of Conduct for the McMurdo Dry Valleys and Additional Guidelines for Scientific Research. The Environmental Code of Conduct comprises general guidelines for activities in the Area, and has already been implemented by both the United States and New Zealand national Antarctic programs. The Additional Guidelines for Scientific Research contains more
specific guidelines for scientific researchers working in various ecosystems including 
streams, lakes, soils, and glaciers.

Next Steps
It is anticipated that a Draft McMurdo Dry Valleys ASMA management plan will be 
completed over the next few months in consultation with key stakeholders and submitted 
to CEP VI.
